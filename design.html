Interaction Design

Design Principles

The five core design principles from Rogers, Sharp & Preece (2023) that guide every interface decision on PeerReview Hub.

Core Principles

Five Principles in Practice

Each principle maps directly from theory (Rogers et al., 2023) to a concrete design decision on the platform.

Principle 1

Visibility

Visible controls lead to greater discoverability. PeerReview Hub's navigation persists across the top of every page showing: Submit Draft · My Reviews · Feedback Received · Dashboard. Primary actions — Submit Draft, Begin Review — are prominently sized and immediately visible on login, so students never need to hunt for what to do next.

Principle 2

Feedback

Every significant action is confirmed immediately. A success notification confirms draft uploads. An autosave indicator appears as reviewers write. A summary confirmation page appears after review submission. Feedback removes anxiety — students never worry whether submissions were received.

Principle 3

Constraints

The Submit Review button is greyed out until all rubric sections are answered. File uploads are limited to PDF, DOCX, and TXT to prevent invalid formats. Time-locked constraints disable submission after the review window closes. Constraints prevent errors before they occur.

Principle 4

Consistency

The same review form structure applies across all assignment types — essay, research proposal, or case study. Navigation paths, buttons, and iconography for action states (active, pending, completed) are identical on every page, minimising the learning time required during a semester of use.

Principle 5

Affordance

File upload areas use a dotted border with a drag-and-drop icon: "Drag and drop your file here, or click to browse." Review text areas are styled to afford typing. Clickable elements change colour on hover. Every interactive element communicates how it should be used without any instruction.

UX Quality

Desirable UX Properties

The interface deliberately pursues: motivating (progress meters, review counts), engaging (guided rubrics, community stats), helpful (contextual cues), rewarding (badges, recognition), and social (anonymous shared space). And it explicitly avoids: frustrating (complex flows), patronising (over-prescriptive copy), boring (flat visual design).

Emotional Design

Norman's Three Levels

Norman (2005) identifies three levels of emotional reaction to design. PeerReview Hub addresses all three explicitly.

01

Visceral

The interface is professional, clean, and academic without being clinically bland. A calming dark colour scheme with teal accents and clear typographic hierarchy creates an immediate sense of trustworthy academic endeavour.

02

Behavioural

The UX must be seamless and efficient. All error states provide both an explanation and a recovery pathway, helping users avoid the "escalating cycle of frustration" Rogers et al. (2023) describe in flawed academic platforms.

03

Reflective

Platform copy emphasises the academic and scholarly value of peer review as a practice. Students feel a sense of ownership over their academic progress — not just task completion, but identity-level investment in their writing.

Expressive Confirmation

"Your draft has been submitted successfully.
Reviewers will provide feedback within 3 days. Good luck!"

Warm, personalised confirmation that recognises the emotional weight of submitting academic work.
